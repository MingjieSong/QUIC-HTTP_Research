After years of drought and restrictions on water use, a series of atmospheric rivers between January and March brought epic amounts of rain and snow to the parched state. The snowpack itself acts as a natural water storage system for California. A drought-busting winter

California‚Äôs ‚Äúwet‚Äù season runs from October to the end of March. That is when much of the state‚Äôs rain and especially snow arrives. This year, the storms kept coming.

Coupled with below-average temperatures, particularly in the Sierra, all that snow was able to pile up.

An enormous snowpack

The Sierra snowpack typically reaches its maximum depth around April 1, when the region‚Äôs climate shifts from the wet to dry season. The snowpack plays a critical role in water storage for the state. Water remains frozen in the mountains until warming spring and summer temperatures gradually release it.

This year‚Äôs snowpack reached 240% of the April average, a level not seen since the 1980s, when the state‚Äôs electronic monitoring system came online.

As of June 7, the snowpack stands at 300% of normal for this time, according to California Department of Water Resources data.

In terms of volume, state officials estimate the snowpack still contains about 9.6 million acre-feet of water. But that does not mean all of it will make its way into state reservoirs. Some of it will be absorbed into the groundwater or by plants. Some will evaporate or sublimate.

Nowhere to go but downhill

As the snow melts in the spring and early summer, the runoff drains down the mountains through the state‚Äôs many watersheds and rivers. Much of that water is captured by California‚Äôs system of reservoirs, the largest of which are located in the north.

DWR calculates the volume of water that would flow through these watersheds if there were no dams, cities or farms capturing or diverting the water. This metric, sometimes called ‚Äúnatural flow,‚Äù takes into account a river basin‚Äôs precipitation patterns, snowpack and reservoir levels to assess how much water a river basin is producing.

‚ÄúThere is a lot of snow up there in various areas, so these watersheds are going to be flowing high for quite some time,‚Äù said Sean de Guzman, DWR‚Äôs manager of snow surveys.

Tracking California’s water supplies

The forecasts are used by reservoir operators to plan for incoming snowmelt. If a large volume of snowmelt is projected to come down, managers may release water from the dam to make room and avoid potential spillover. For that reason, the forecast may not be a one-to-one comparison to actual river and stream conditions.

This spring, the Sacramento River hydrologic region saw an immediate peak in runoff due to the massive volumes of rain. Because the area is much lower in elevation than other watersheds, much of its water comes from rain rather than snow. Hydrologic regions are essentially drainage basins, contained by the state‚Äôs mountain ranges and consisting of a number of watersheds and rivers.

Forecast: flooding

While most of California‚Äôs rivers drain in the ocean, those in the Tulare Lake region do not.

This winter‚Äôs incessant rains filled much of the state‚Äôs depleted reservoirs but also inundated a massive area just west of Corcoran. The lakebed, drained of water a century ago to make room for agricultural fields, reemerged during this year‚Äôs storms.

State officials believe flooding in the region has largely peaked due to continued cool temperatures in the mountains.

At the end of last month, about 110,000 acres of land in Tulare Lake were flooded, an area larger than the city of Bakersfield. That translates to about 580,000 acre-feet of water.

State models indicate the lake could expand another thousand acres. In a lower probability, wet scenario that number could rise to 118,000 acres.

The land near Corcoran is made up of dense clay, making it hard for standing water to be absorbed into the ground. The area could remain flooded for years.
